It's a strange thing to look back at a codebase you worked in and finally understand what was bothering you about it. Not because the understanding is particularly satisfying, but because you remember exactly how it felt to sense that something was wrong, say something about it, and watch it go nowhere.

This is that article. A reconstruction from memory and reflection, written with the benefit of distance, a clearer head, and a few years of additional context.

## Setting the scene

It was 2020. The world had just moved indoors. I was a frontend engineer working on a product built on a stack that, on paper, had a kind of radical simplicity to it.

There was no backend.

Well, not exactly. There was Microsoft SQL Server, and there was a thin C# layer that acted purely as a router, mapping API routes to stored procedures. All business logic lived in the database, written in T-SQL stored procedures. Some of it was written in .NET assembly code deployed directly into SQL Server via SQLCLR.

The frontend talked to routes. The routes called stored procedures. The database did everything else.

The architect was genuinely brilliant in his domain. Deeply passionate about SQL Server in a way that most engineers are never passionate about anything. And he had a compelling argument for why this made sense.

## The argument sounded clever

The pitch was essentially this: a standard web application requires three layers: a database, a backend, and a frontend. That means three languages, three deployment concerns, three places for bugs to hide. By collapsing the backend into the database, you eliminate a layer. Two languages instead of three. Less to maintain. Less to go wrong.

It sounded pragmatic. It sounded like someone had thought carefully about accidental complexity and decided to cut it.

I was a frontend engineer. My scope was the frontend. But some things were visible even from there: scaling would be a problem, the database was doing too many jobs at once, the environments didn't match reliably. I raised these things. They didn't land. There was no process for them to land in, and the person who needed to hear them wasn't particularly interested in hearing them.

So I did what most engineers do in that situation. I kept my head down and did my job. I was also, at the time, heads down in a migration from AngularJS to Angular 2, which brought with it TypeScript, RxJS, reactive programming concepts, and an entirely new mental model for frontend development.

I was surviving, not reflecting. It was 2020.

## What I understand now

The argument had a fundamental flaw, and it took me longer than I'd like to admit to articulate it fully.

**Removing a layer doesn't remove its complexity. It just removes its address.**

The business logic didn't disappear when the backend was removed. It moved. Some of it moved into the database. Some of it moved into the frontend. Some of it moved into the gap between environments where things worked in staging but not in production for reasons nobody could fully explain.

This is the distinction between essential and incidental complexity. Essential complexity is irreducible: it exists because the problem is complex. Your business rules are complicated because your business is complicated. You cannot make that go away by changing where it lives. Incidental complexity is what you add on top through poor tooling, wrong abstractions, or mismatched layers.

The architect thought he was removing essential complexity, an entire layer. What he was actually doing was keeping all the essential complexity and adding significant incidental complexity by housing it somewhere it didn't belong.

## Where the complexity actually went

**It went into the response shapes.**

Without a layer to transform and shape data before it reached the frontend, the raw output of SQL Server result sets arrived directly in the client. Nested arrays, data wrapped in data, structures that reflected how the database stored things rather than what the UI actually needed. Every response had to be remapped in the frontend before it could be used. The transformation work didn't disappear. It just became my problem.

**It went into frontend orchestration.**

A backend exists partly to aggregate multiple data sources and return exactly what the UI needs in a single round trip. Without it, the frontend had to manage dependencies between requests: call this endpoint, take the result, pass it into the next call, handle failures at each step. This logic had no proper home. It lived in RxJS pipe chains that were difficult to read, difficult to test, and brittle against changes in the stored procedures they depended on.

**It went into the environments.**

Deploying a stateless application server is straightforward: containers are identical, environments can be made to match. But when logic lives in the database, the database carries both state and behavior. Schema versions, stored procedure versions, SQLCLR assembly versions, server configurations: these diverge over time in ways that are subtle and hard to diff. We had staging environments, but they didn't reliably match production. Something could work perfectly in one and fail quietly in the other. This is called environment drift, and it gets worse with every deployment.

## The silence was a signal

There's a heuristic I've come to trust since then.

If something is genuinely clever and good, the industry tends to converge on it. Patterns that work survive and spread. Patterns that seem clever but contain fatal flaws get tried, get abandoned, and that knowledge propagates: not always loudly, but consistently. The mainstream not doing something is data, not ignorance.

Storing business logic in the database was done heavily in the 90s and early 2000s. The industry moved away from it collectively, over time, as the problems became undeniable. The architect wasn't being innovative. He was rediscovering a discarded pattern and dressing it up in modern tooling.

There's a principle called Chesterton's Fence: don't tear down a fence until you understand why it was built. The inverse applies here. Don't build a fence that nobody else is building until you understand why everyone stopped.

## On scope and the limits of a single role

Something I've made peace with since then: it wasn't my job to perform a full architectural autopsy.

I was a frontend engineer. I could see the symptoms closest to me: the awkward response shapes, the chained requests, the things that made my daily work harder than it needed to be. I noticed some of the larger structural issues too, the scaling concerns, the resource contention, the environment problems. I mentioned them. But tracing every issue back to its root cause, building a comprehensive case, articulating the full theoretical framework for why the architecture was wrong: that wasn't my scope, and I didn't have the bandwidth for it.

I think engineers sometimes feel guilty about this. Like they should have fought harder, gone deeper, been more articulate in the moment. But there's a difference between having responsibilities and having unlimited jurisdiction. A frontend engineer raising architectural concerns is already going beyond the expected. Not having a fully formed rebuttal ready isn't a failure: it's just the reality of working within a role, during a pandemic, while learning several new things simultaneously.

Understanding it fully took distance. That's what hindsight is for.

## The organizational part

The technical problems were real, but they didn't emerge in a vacuum.

There were no staff or principal engineers. No RFC process, no architecture review, no structured way for the team to challenge decisions before they became permanent. But the deeper issue wasn't just the absence of process. It was that feedback, when offered, didn't go anywhere.

That's worth saying clearly, because it's different from an architect who simply didn't seek input. Not seeking feedback is a passive failure: an oversight, perhaps a blind spot. Not receiving feedback is an active one. It means the information arrived and was filtered out. It means the system was closed not by accident but by disposition.

This matters because process alone doesn't fix it. You can have RFCs and architecture reviews and all the right rituals, and if the person at the center has decided in advance that they're right, the process becomes theatre. What actually protects a codebase from one person's certainty is a combination of process and genuine intellectual openness. Both have to be present.

Passion is not the same as correctness. Deep expertise in one tool can narrow your vision as much as it sharpens it. And when the person making foundational decisions is also the person least interested in being questioned, the risk doesn't just come from their blind spots. It comes from the fact that nobody else can see around them either.

## What I'd tell myself

A few things, if I could go back:

**You saw real things.** The scaling concerns, the resource contention, the environment drift: those weren't vague anxieties. They were accurate observations. The fact that you couldn't build a complete theoretical framework around them in the moment doesn't make them less real.

**Raising it was enough.** You said something. It didn't land. That's not on you. Your job wasn't to win the argument: it was to surface the concern. You did that.

**Count the complexity, not the layers.** A simpler-looking architecture isn't necessarily simpler. Ask where the complexity went, not whether it's visible.

**The industry's silence is a signal.** Novel isn't the same as good. If something is genuinely new, ask whether it's new because it's better, or new because everyone else already tried it.

**You were surviving.** It was 2020, you were learning three new things at once, the world was on fire. Picking it apart later, clearly and honestly, is its own kind of thoroughness. It just runs on a different timeline.

Hindsight is 2020. Sometimes literally.
